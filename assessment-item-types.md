## Argumentation Assessment (Section 3, items 25–51)

The team's Spring 2026 categorization uses three concepts: **Identification**, **Arg Alignment**, and **Critique/Evaluation**. Working definitions:

- **Identification** — the argument is already articulated in writing (a character states their claim/evidence/reasoning out loud); the student picks the right sentence as the claim, the evidence, or the reasoning.
- **Arg Alignment** — the student must determine which **argument piece** (claim or evidence) **fits with a representation of student thinking** (typically a picture). The "alignment" is between an argument piece and the representation of thinking — not whether a picture appears on the page. Example: items 32–35 show pictures of what two students think happened to dissolved sugar; the question presents argument pieces and asks which one fits each student's picture.
- **Critique/Evaluation** — the student evaluates which of two arguments is stronger and articulates why.

The codebook tags every Identification *and* Arg Alignment item with the single label `Identification`; the team's split is finer-grained than the codebook itself records.

## Notes on the Categorization

1. **Atmospheric Water as its own category.** The codebook keeps `atmospheric water` (evaporation, condensation, particle-level phase change) as a distinct sub-tag from `water cycle`. The curriculum team's Spring 2026 mapping treats Atmospheric Water as a top-level concept on equal footing with Water Cycle, so this document does the same. Jim's original question used four water-systems categories without naming Atmospheric Water; folding Atmospheric Water into Water Cycle (since evaporation and condensation are the surface↔atmosphere legs of the cycle, and are also the substance of MHS objective W5.1, "explain movement of water between surface and atmospheric systems") recovers Jim's framing.

2. **Identification vs. Arg Alignment.** The codebook tags every item across the Identification and Arg Alignment buckets with the single label `Identification`. The curriculum team applies a finer-grained split:
   - **Identification** = the argument is already articulated in writing; the student picks out which sentence is the claim, evidence, or reasoning.
   - **Arg Alignment** = the student must determine which argument piece (claim or evidence) fits with a representation of student thinking. For items 32–35, the pictures show what two students think happened to dissolved sugar, and the question presents argument pieces that the student must align with each picture. Items 42–44 follow the same pattern with a different scenario. Items 25, 39, and 49 align argument pieces (or contradicting statements/evidence) with a representation.

3. **Pre/post symmetry.** The pre-test and post-test use the same items in the same order with the same codebook tags, so this index applies identically to both.

4. **Item 25 (S3Q1)** is a four-row matrix item; the codebook decomposes it into S3Q1_1 through S3Q1_4. Counted as one survey question above.
