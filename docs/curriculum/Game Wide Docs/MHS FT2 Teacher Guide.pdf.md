                                  Standards - Overview of Science Curriculum


  OVERVIEW OF MISSION HYDROSCI SCIENCE CURRICULUM
  Mission HydroSci (MHS) teaches earth science concepts at a level at is appropriate for middle school students. Earth Science
  is an integrated science, bringing together chemistry, biology, and physics as they apply to the workings of the Earth. The
  applied and often visual nature of Earth Science helps students make connections to their lives and communities. The
  curriculum of MHS focuses on the water systems component of Earth Science. MHS consists of various curriculum units are
  each designed to teach a major topic within water systems. These major topics include: surface water, water pollution,
  groundwater, and atmospheric water. As students play through MHS, they will complete tasks that require knowledge of
  water systems. These tasks range from findings the source of pollution within a watershed to navigating through an
  underground puzzle using their knowledge of evaporation and condensation.

  In addition to teaching students about water systems content, the MHS curriculum also focuses on building students’
  argumentation skills. Within each unit, students are given the opportunity to formulate an argument by selecting
  appropriate and claim, reasoning and evidence. Within each unit, player will collect data about the planet and use their
  collected data to support their arguments. Throughout the game players will argue about a range of topics from where to
  set up base camp based on the size of surrounding watersheds to the cause of a flood in an underground bunker based on
  data about soil profile. The MHS curriculum emphasizes an understanding of water systems knowledge, as well as applying
  science knowledge in the argumentation tasks.

   through the multiple pathways of the hydrologic cycle. Examples of models can be conceptual or physical.]

   6-8.ESS3.C.2 Apply scientific principles to design a method for monitoring and minimizing a human impact on the
   environment. [Clarification Statement: Examples of the design process include examining human environmental impacts,
   assessing the kinds of solutions that are feasible, and designing and evaluating solutions that could reduce that impact.
   Examples of human impacts can include water usage (such as the withdrawal of water from streams and aquifers or the
   construction of dams and levees), land usage (such as urban development, agriculture, or the removal of wetlands), and
   pollution (such as of the air, water, or land).]

   6-8.ESS3.A Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence for how the uneven distributions of Earth’s mineral,
   energy, and groundwater resources are the result of past and current geoscience processes and human activity.
    [Clarification Statement: Emphasis is on how these resources are limited and typically non-renewable, and how their
   distributions are significantly changing as a result of removal by humans. Examples of uneven distributions of resources as a
   result of past processes include but are not limited to petroleum (locations of the burial of organic marine sediments and
   subsequent geologic traps), metal ores (locations of past volcanic and hydrothermal activity associated with subduction
   zones), and soil (locations of active weathering and/or deposition of rock).]

   6-8.ESS3.C.1 Analyze data to define the relationship for how increases in human population and per-capita consumption of

        ABOUT THIS GUIDE
        This guide has been designed to help you implement the MHS curriculum in your classroom. You may find the
        table of contents to be a useful resource to get a sense for the information that the guide contains. This guide
        consists of three main pieces, which are the Intro, Unit guides, and appendices. Each of these three pieces are
        described in more detail below.



    INTRODUCTION
 The introductory section of the guide provides an overview to the MHS curriculum. This includes a summary of the curriculum
 topics that are covered within the game, as well as listing out specific Missouri Learning Standards (MLS) and Next Generation
 Science Standards (NGSS) that are covered in MHS. You will also find a chart that outlines pacing suggestions for
 implementing the curriculum. To accommodate both 45 min and 90 min scheduling, there are versions of the pacing guide for
 both situations. In an effort to orient teachers to the story of MHS, this part of the guide also includes an overview of the
 characters and the story of MHS. This section concludes with tips for getting started, which concludes an overview of the
 controls and troubleshooting tips.



    UNIT GUIDES
 Perhaps the most useful part of this guide for teachers will be the unit specific guides. MHS consists of six units that students
 will complete in order. The main goal of the unit guides is to provide teachers with walkthrough of each unit, so they are able
 to help students that get stuck. Each unit guide includes the list of quests and tasks that students will need to complete in
 order to progress in the game. Each unit guide includes area maps for each unit that includes all of the points of interest in the
 unit. This will allow teachers to help students that cannot figure out where they are supposed to go. Each unit guide also
 provides an overview of essential science ideas that are covered within the unit, as well identifies common misconceptions that
 students have about the unit topic.




    APPENDICES
 The final portion of the guide consists of supplemental materials. These materials consist of a glossary, index, as well as links to
 supplemental materials. For each unit of MHS there is accompanying powerpoint file that can be accessed through the canvas
 course. These powerpoint files are designed to provide discussion questions that students can engage in to help reinforce
 important topics that were covered in each unit of MHS. These follow up activities are included in the pacing guide for MHS.
