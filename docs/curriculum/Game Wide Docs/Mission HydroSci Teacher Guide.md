                                        Unit Guides
Unit guides provide teachers with a step-by-step walkthrough of each unit to help students who get
stuck. Each unit guide includes a list of quests and tasks that students must complete to progress in
   the game, and a map noting points of interest. Teachers can also find an overview of essential
 science ideas covered within the unit, common student questions, and probing questions teachers
                 can ask to help students get the most out of the curricular content.

                 Overview of MHS and Pilot Study
This guide is a draft of the potential teacher guidebook for helping students play through
Mission HydroSci (MHS). It is intended as an overview of gameplay and helps you understand
where students are in the game and work with them to resolve issues and maximize the
benefits of playing MHS. You can use this guide to see what students are trying to
accomplish during gameplay and understand the context of their activity. If you see errors or
ways to improve this document to support teachers, please let the MHS team know about
your observations and ideas.

Mission HydroSci (MHS) teaches earth science concepts at a level appropriate for middle
school students. Earth Science is an integrated science, bringing together chemistry, biology,
and physics as they apply to the workings of the Earth. The applied and often visual nature of
Earth Science helps students make connections to their lives and communities. The
curriculum of MHS focuses on the water systems component of Earth Science. MHS consists
of various curriculum units designed to teach a major topic within water systems. These
major topics include: surface water, water pollution, groundwater, and atmospheric water. As
students play through MHS, they will complete tasks that require knowledge of water
systems. These tasks range from finding the source of pollution within a watershed to
navigating through an underground puzzle using their knowledge of evaporation and
condensation.

In addition to teaching students about water systems content, the MHS curriculum also
focuses on building students’ argumentation skills. Within each unit, students are given the
opportunity to formulate an argument by selecting an appropriate claim, reasoning, and
evidence. Within each unit, the player will collect data about the planet and use this collected
data to support their arguments. Throughout the game, players will argue about a range of
topics, from where to set up base camp based on the size of surrounding watersheds to the
cause of a flood in an underground bunker based on data about soil profiles. The MHS
curriculum emphasizes an understanding of water systems knowledge, as well as applying
science knowledge in the argumentation tasks.

Argumentation System

The MHS curriculum strongly emphasizes argumentation, and the argumentation system
allows players to construct an argument by selecting a claim, reasoning, and evidence. Each
unit has an argumentation task that allows students to build arguments with non-player
characters in the game to move the story forward. The argumentation system also provides
feedback to help players improve their arguments.

                                         How to Use
    The argumentation system can only be used to build an argument at key points in the
 storyline where players are instructed to do so, but students can view the information they
 gather in-game in this menu anytime as they progress. As players collect claims, evidence,
    reasoning, and backing info in the game, these items will populate the argumentation
                                           system.

 Once all required information is collected and players are instructed to build an argument,
  they can drag and drop the claim, evidence, and reasoning orbs onto the central rings to
   construct a complete argument. To check if an argument is correct, players should click
     "submit." After submission, players are provided with feedback on their arguments.

 and meet up with the rest of the Mission Hydrosci team. Once reunited, the team wants to set up a
    base camp near the largest watershed in the region but can't agree on whether the eastern or

  their knowledge of water systems to determine which data is important. To finish the unit, players
           build a convincing argument to help the team decide where to place base camp.



                            Underlying Facts & Principles
     A watershed is an area of land over which water flows through a connected series of
     waterways (e.g., tributaries, streams, and rivers).
     The size of a watershed relates to the amount of water that flows through it. More water flows
     through larger watersheds.
     Smaller watersheds may be nested within larger watersheds.
     Topography determines the shape and size of a watershed. Water flows from higher elevation
     to lower elevation because of gravity, and topography is a way of representing changes in
     elevation across a landscape. Topographic features also can interrupt or redirect the flow of
     water thereby shaping the path of waterways.
     There is a predictable relationship among tributaries, streams, rivers and watersheds.
     Tributaries at relatively high elevations are small and feed into progressively larger streams,
     which feed into progressively larger rivers.(Endorheic watersheds are a special type of
     watershed which drains to a closed lake rather than an ocean—The Great Salt Lake is an
     example. Having students work with endorheic watersheds is NOT a focus of MHS.)

                                     Unit Description
  In this unit, Tera asks for help finding crates containing video equipment that came down during
the crash. After finding the supplies, the player must use their hoverboard to drop the heavy crates
into one of two rivers. In order to successfully complete this task, they must use their knowledge of
     surface water flow to choose the correct river. After the player returns the equipment, Tera
      discovers that the river water she's been using to water her crops is polluted with a toxic
   substance, killing the team's food main food source. The player is tasked with placing pollution
sensors in the river in order to collect evidence to locate the source of the pollution. After collecting
 enough evidence, they must build a successful argument to convince Tera of their findings so the
                                       pollutant can be dealt with.

Since the majority of the seeds were already affected by the pollutant, Jasper, Tera, and the player
 visit an alien facility in an attempt to find a nutrient source that aids plant growth. Inside the alien
   ruins, the player must solve puzzles in order to restore the flow of a mysterious supernutrient.
 After the flow of supernutrients is restored, the player uses their knowledge of water flow to help
Tera choose locations to plant seeds along the river so they will benefit from the flowing nutrients.

                       Underlying Facts & Principles

Materials that dissolve in water will move with water while the water remains in a liquid state.
Molecules of a dissolved solid are thoroughly mixed with water molecules and cannot be
separated by physical means [cannot be separated by filtration].
Chemical characteristics of water [solvent] and the dissolved solid [solute] are not altered
when a solution is formed.
Substances dissolved in water will be carried by liquid water from place to place.
Gravity and topography govern where water flows in a watershed resulting in particular
connected patterns of water flow across a watershed.
    The pattern of water movement will be determined by the topography of the watershed.
    This governs where surface pollutants will travel
