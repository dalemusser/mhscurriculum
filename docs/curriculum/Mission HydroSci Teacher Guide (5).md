                                       Introduction
 The introductory section of this guide provides an overview of the MHS curriculum and suggestions
  for classroom implementation. It includes a summary of the Next Generation Science Standards
(NGSS) addressed by gameplay and classroom pacing suggestions. This section also includes tips for
        getting started, an overview of controls and game systems, and troubleshooting tips.


                                        Unit Guides
Unit guides provide teachers with a step-by-step walkthrough of each unit to help students who get
stuck. Each unit guide includes a list of quests and tasks that students must complete to progress in
   the game, and a map noting points of interest. Teachers can also find an overview of essential
 science ideas covered within the unit, common student questions, and probing questions teachers
                 can ask to help students get the most out of the curricular content.

                 Overview of MHS and Pilot Study
This guide is a draft of the potential teacher guidebook for helping students play through
Mission HydroSci (MHS). It is intended as an overview of gameplay and helps you understand
where students are in the game and work with them to resolve issues and maximize the
benefits of playing MHS. You can use this guide to see what students are trying to
accomplish during gameplay and understand the context of their activity. If you see errors or
ways to improve this document to support teachers, please let the MHS team know about
your observations and ideas.

Mission HydroSci (MHS) teaches earth science concepts at a level appropriate for middle
school students. Earth Science is an integrated science, bringing together chemistry, biology,
and physics as they apply to the workings of the Earth. The applied and often visual nature of
Earth Science helps students make connections to their lives and communities. The
curriculum of MHS focuses on the water systems component of Earth Science. MHS consists
of various curriculum units designed to teach a major topic within water systems. These
major topics include: surface water, water pollution, groundwater, and atmospheric water. As
students play through MHS, they will complete tasks that require knowledge of water
systems. These tasks range from finding the source of pollution within a watershed to
navigating through an underground puzzle using their knowledge of evaporation and
condensation.

In addition to teaching students about water systems content, the MHS curriculum also
focuses on building students’ argumentation skills. Within each unit, students are given the
opportunity to formulate an argument by selecting an appropriate claim, reasoning, and
evidence. Within each unit, the player will collect data about the planet and use this collected
data to support their arguments. Throughout the game, players will argue about a range of
topics, from where to set up base camp based on the size of surrounding watersheds to the
cause of a flood in an underground bunker based on data about soil profiles. The MHS
curriculum emphasizes an understanding of water systems knowledge, as well as applying
science knowledge in the argumentation tasks.

                                            Standards
Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS)
Earth and Space Science Standards

6-8.ESS2.C.1 Design and develop a model to describe the cycling of water through Earth's systems driven
by energy from the sun and the force of gravity. [Clarification Statement: Emphasis is on the ways water
changes its state as it moves through the multiple pathways of the hydrologic cycle. Examples of models
can be conceptual or physical.] (MLS:6-8.ESS2.C.1)

6-8.ESS3.C.2 Apply scientific principles to design a method for monitoring and minimizing a human impact
on the environment. [Clarification Statement: Examples of the design process include examining human
environmental impacts, assessing the kinds of solutions that are feasible, and designing and evaluating
solutions that could reduce that impact. Examples of human impacts can include water usage (such as the
withdrawal of water from streams and aquifers or the construction of dams and levees), land usage (such
as urban development, agriculture, or the removal of wetlands), and pollution (such as of the air, water, or
land).] (MLS: 6-8.ESS3.C.2)

6-8.ESS3.A Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence for how the uneven distributions of
Earth’s mineral, energy, and groundwater resources are the result of past and current geoscience
processes and human activity. [Clarification Statement: Emphasis is on how these resources are limited
and typically non-renewable, and how their distributions are significantly changing as a result of removal
by humans. Examples of uneven distributions of resources as a result of past processes include but are
not limited to petroleum (locations of the burial of organic marine sediments and subsequent geologic
traps), metal ores (locations of past volcanic and hydrothermal activity associated with subduction zones),
and soil (locations of active weathering and/or deposition of rock).] (MLS: 6-8.ESS3.A.1)

6-8.ESS3.C.1 )(Partial Coverage) Analyze data to define the relationship for how increases in human
population and per-capita consumption of natural resources impact Earth's systems. [Clarification
Statement: Examples of data include grade-appropriate databases on human populations and the rates of
consumption of food and natural resources (such as freshwater, mineral, and energy). Examples of
impacts can include changes to the appearance, composition, and structure of Earth’s systems as well as
the rates at which they change.] (MLS: 6-8.ESS3.C.1 ]


Engineering, Technology, and Application of Science

6-8.ETS1.A Define the criteria and constraints of a design problem with sufficient precision to ensure a
successful solution, taking into account relevant scientific principles and potential impacts on people and
the natural environment that may limit possible solutions. (MLS: 6-8.ETS1.A)

Registering Player Input
Make sure you have clicked into the game window to register controls.

Advancing Dialogue
Players can advance dialogue by clicking "Continue" or pressing Enter. Some scenes
may only respond to clicks.




Making a Dialogue Choice
Players choose a response to a dialogue choice by clicking on the desired option. If the player
presses Enter the first option will be auto-selected.




Moving on to the Next Unit
To continue to the next unit after completing a unit players can click the “Load Unit” instead
of returning to Strata Hub.

Argumentation System

The MHS curriculum strongly emphasizes argumentation, and the argumentation system
allows players to construct an argument by selecting a claim, reasoning, and evidence. Each
unit has an argumentation task that allows students to build arguments with non-player
characters in the game to move the story forward. The argumentation system also provides
feedback to help players improve their arguments.

                                         How to Use
    The argumentation system can only be used to build an argument at key points in the
 storyline where players are instructed to do so, but students can view the information they
                   gather in-game in this menu anytime as they progress.

    Clicking a claim, evidence, or reasoning orb will add it to the argument. To check if an
  argument is correct, players should click "submit." After submission, players are provided
                             with feedback on their arguments.

                                      Unit Description
In the first unit of the game, the player is introduced to their mission onboard the spaceship Copernicus.
 The Copernicus is hovering above the mysterious planet WAT-247, where the Mission Hydrosci team is
  planning to conduct research. After a brief orientation, the player encounters their first snag- Mission
   Control is uncertain if WAT-247 is a suitable place to set up base. They are not convinced that it has
fresh water. The player visits the other cadets onboard to collect the necessary pieces of information to
 make an argument to convince Mission Control that WAT-247 does have fresh water. After successfully
   completing the argument, the Mission Hydrosci team encounters a strange alien phenomenon that
causes the ship's core power system to overload. The player runs to an escape pod and is able to get off
                              the ship before it crashes into the planet below.




                          Underlying Facts & Principles

        Scientific arguments seek to answer a driving question- an unanswered question about
        something in the environment.

        Scientific arguments are made of of three parts- a claim, evidence, and reasoning

                            Underlying Facts & Principles
     A watershed is an area of land over which water flows through a connected series of
     waterways (e.g., tributaries, streams, and rivers).
     The size of a watershed relates to the amount of water that flows through it. More water flows
     through larger watersheds.
     Smaller watersheds may be nested within larger watersheds.
     Topography determines the shape and size of a watershed. Water flows from higher elevation
     to lower elevation because of gravity, and topography is a way of representing changes in
     elevation across a landscape. Topographic features also can interrupt or redirect the flow of
     water thereby shaping the path of waterways.
     There is a predictable relationship among tributaries, streams, rivers and watersheds.
     Tributaries at relatively high elevations are small and feed into progressively larger streams,
     which feed into progressively larger rivers.(Endorheic watersheds are a special type of
     watershed which drains to a closed lake rather than an ocean—The Great Salt Lake is an
     example. Having students work with endorheic watersheds is NOT a focus of MHS.)

                                      Unit Description
    In unit 5 of MHS the player will learn about the movement of water through the water cycle
    focusing on changes of state within atmospheric water and surface water. This includes the
processes of evaporation and condensation and how they relate to the movement of water. Players
   will learn about factors that impact the rate of evaporation and condensation, and engage in
    argumentation about this phenomenon. The argumentation sequence in Unit 5 involves the
           examination of a faulty claim, and the player must provide a counter argument.




                           Underlying Facts & Principles
 Atmospheric water is part of the water cycle.
 Water enters the atmosphere through evaporation from surface water, transpiration from plants,
 and evaporation from animals.
 The movement of water into the atmosphere requires a phase change [liquid state into a gaseous
 state].
 Changes in phase refer to water vapor condensing into a liquid or liquid water evaporating to form
 water vapor or gaseous water.
 Water is able to evaporate while other substances, such as salts, are not.
 Changes in altitude cause a change in temperature(energy) and therefore phase.

For each unit of the MHS curriculum, there is an included suggested follow up activity.

Follow-up activities are mainly opportunities for students to discuss concepts & ideas from the game
in a different setting. Each follow-up activity has been designed to help students make sense of the more
difficult concepts and ideas from each unit, and they are listed below.

Instructions for Using the Follow-Up Materials
The follow-up activities have been designed to give students the opportunity to review key concepts from
the MHS curriculum. Within the MHS pacing guide, there are suggestions for when to use each follow-up
activity. Each follow-up activity is a PowerPoint presentation that consists of multiple discussion questions
that allow students to draw on their knowledge of the topics covered in the MHS curriculum. Each follow-
up activity has been designed to support student learning of specific MHS units, as they reinforce topics
covered in specific units of MHS.

We intended the follow-up PowerPoint presentations to be used to stimulate class discussion about the
questions on the slides. The class discussion can either be whole group, or small group depending on the
size of the class, but we do recommend giving students the opportunity to discuss these questions with
their peers. Within each slide, there are included speaker notes that indicate the correct answer to the
questions, as well as provide an explanation that you can share with your students. The speaker notes also
indicate area topics that are difficult for students to understand where they may need more assistance.
As reflected in the MHS pacing guide, the follow-up activities have been designed as quick discussions
that can be completed in around 15 min.
