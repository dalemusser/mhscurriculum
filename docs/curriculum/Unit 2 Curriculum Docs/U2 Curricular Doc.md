**Unit 2 Curricular Doc: Watershed**

**Topic:** Landscape-scale flow of surface water through a watershed.

**Learning Objectives**

**Water Systems**

> Relate the relative size of a watershed to the relative volume of water that flows through it.
>
> **Argumentation**
>
> Support a claim with evidence.

**Driving Question:** How does the amount of water output from a watershed predict the size of the watershed?

**Underlying Facts & Principles**

- A watershed is an area of land over which water flows through a connected series of waterways (e.g., tributaries, streams, and rivers).

- The size of a watershed relates to the amount of water that flows through it. More water flows through larger watersheds.

- Smaller watersheds may be nested within larger watersheds.

- Topography determines the shape and size of a watershed. Water flows from higher elevation to lower elevation because of gravity, and topography is a way of representing changes in elevation across a landscape. Topographic features also can interrupt or redirect the flow of water thereby shaping the path of waterways.

- There is a predictable relationship among tributaries, streams, rivers and watersheds. Tributaries at relatively high elevations are small and feed into progressively larger streams, which feed into progressively larger rivers. The largest part of a river is the *mouth*, which connects the terrestrial watershed to an ocean. (Endorheic watersheds are a special type of watershed which drains to a closed lake rather than an ocean—The Great Salt Lake is an example. Having students work with endorheic watersheds is NOT a focus of MHS.)

| Rivers flow south. Some students consider southerly direction as necessarily lower elevation. | Rivers flow downhill regardless of direction. The direction of river flow is determined by the topography of the watershed. Rivers flow from a smaller origin to a larger mouth which typically opens on a large body of water, such as an ocean or sea. |
| Rivers are only found in natural or rural environments. | Rivers exist in all types of landscapes including: natural, rural, urban, and suburban. While the headwaters of a river are always at a higher elevation than the mouth, the headwaters of a river do not have to be located within a mountainous region. |

**Extension Activities**

- The Leaders of the Water Harvest Mission need to choose between 2 or more continents to send a water harvester. Data are presented on the continents: area of continents, landscape maps, salinity? A character in the prompt chooses the continent with the most landmass. Another character chooses the continent with the biggest watershed. Player has to choose a solution and create an argument to justify that decision.

- Model and next, determine the path of the water through the watershed. This activity would provide greater evidence for the interconnectedness of streams and rivers within a watershed. \[Project Wet: curriculum and Activity Guide. The water Course, p.129-32\].

- Students study a city map and a topographic map of the same area. The goal is for students to identify bodies of water near the school grounds. Next, students are challenged to determine the creeks, streams, rivers, lakes, etc. into which the body of water drains and, by doing so, identify the watershed in which the school is located. \[[<u>Link</u>](http://bcn.boulder.co.us/basin/learning/mappingteacher.html)\]

- Students could continue to investigate watersheds through the Potomac Highlands Watershed. This is a virtual experience which engages students with multiple aspects of watersheds. \[[<u>Link</u>](http://www.cacaponinstitute.org/middle.htm)\]
