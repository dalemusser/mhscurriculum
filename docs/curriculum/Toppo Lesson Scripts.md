**U4 Soil Infiltration (5th Grade) (<span class="mark">Flesch–Kincaid: 5.6)</span>**

Good morning, cadets. In today’s survival skills series, we’ll talk about soil infiltration. Water from the surface can infiltrate, or flow, through soil and into the ground. Water will infiltrate down through soil layers due to gravity.

The rate at which water moves depends largely on the type of soil through which it passes. Under a microscope, soil shows particles that vary in size. As a result, the space between its particles varies. The greater the space between particles, the easier it is for water to pass through. Water moves slowly through soil that has small pore spaces. It flows quickly through soil with larger pore spaces.

Soil with smaller particles has more surface area. This helps water cling to it and resist gravity. As a result, water moves slowly and stays in the soil longer. Soil with bigger particles has a hard time holding water. Water moves fast through the gaps between these particles.

As water moves deeper into the ground, it filters through layers of soil. This removes pollutants. As a result, the water is cleaner. Water will eventually meet bedrock. When this happens, it can’t flow down any further. The pore space in the area below is too small. At this point, water begins to collect and form large storehouses of water.

Soil is “saturated” when water fills every space between the soil particles. We call the uppermost surface of the saturated area the “water table.” The soil below the water table contains a lot of groundwater. This can be helpful when other water sources aren’t available.

To reach this water, wells can be dug deep into the ground to reach the water table. The deeper a well is, the cleaner its water. This happens because the water has been filtered through more soil layers. Be careful not to dig too far, though! If you hit bedrock, you'll be below the water table.

When you can’t find water on the surface, try looking underground. You may just save the team from dehydration!

**U5 Evaporation/Condensation (5th Grade)(<span class="mark">Flesch–Kincaid: 7.3)</span>**

Good morning, cadets. Today in our survival skills series, we'll be covering evaporation and condensation. You can find water in several different forms or states. It can be a liquid, a solid, or a gas.

To change forms, water must have energy added or removed. This often occurs by changing its temperature.

Let's take a moment and look at the particles of each state of water. Notice how the particles in the solid form are close together. In the liquid state, particles are a bit farther apart. In the gaseous state, they are even more spread out.

When ice is solid, its particles are close together. When you add energy, the temperature goes up. The particles gain energy, move faster, and spread out. If the particles gain enough energy, they can transition from a liquid to a gas. We call this process of a liquid turning into a gas evaporation.

The opposite process in which a gas turns into a liquid is called condensation. When water vapor cools, its particles lose energy. They move more slowly and start to clump together. If they continue to lose energy, the water vapor will eventually turn back into a liquid.

**U5 Water Cycle (5th Grade)(<span class="mark">Flesch–Kincaid: 6)</span>**

Good morning, cadets. Today in our survival skills series, we'll be talking about the water cycle.

The water cycle happens all around us. Surface water from oceans, lakes, and rivers can evaporate. When the sun shines, it heats the water, leading to evaporation.

As water vapor rises, it meets cooler temperatures in the atmosphere. Eventually, it will get cold enough for water to condense. Then, it will turn into liquid and fall from the sky as rain.

Groundwater is also part of the water cycle. Surface water can infiltrate through soil to become groundwater. Groundwater can seep to the surface in areas where the water table is very high. It can also return to the surface through springs and even with the help of plants.

Water evaporates, condenses, and infiltrates in a continuous cycle. This means the total amount of water on a planet like WAT247 stays the same. This will still be true even after millions of years!

On a small scale we can see this cycle at work inside a solar desalinator. This is a simple tool that uses the energy from the sun to turn saltwater into freshwater.

Some are simple and some are complex, but they all work the same way. First, salty water enters a chamber. Then the sun heats up the salt water. When it gets hot enough it will start to evaporate leaving the salt behind.

After the water evaporates, the vapor touches a cool surface like glass or plastic. This causes the water to become colder and condense. The condensation drips down into a storage area where you can collect it as fresh water.

A desalinator can keep you alive if you’re ever shipwrecked. Not that MHS brought any boats.

**U5 Evaporation/Condensation (8th Grade)**

Good morning, cadets, Captain Toppo here. Today in our survival skills series, we'll be covering evaporation and condensation. Water can be found in several different forms or states. Sometimes it’s found in a liquid form as we’ve discussed in past lessons. Another form is that of a solid.

In order for water to change forms, energy must be added or removed. This often occurs by changing its temperature.

Notice how the particles in the solid form are close together. In the liquid state, the particles are a bit farther apart, and in the gaseous state, the particles are even farther apart.

Starting with water in its solid state, ice, the particles are close together, but when energy is added the temperature rises and the particles gain energy and begin to move faster and spread out. If the particles gain enough energy, they can transition from a liquid to a gas.

The process of a liquid turning into a gas is called evaporation. Inversely, the process of a gas turning into a liquid is called condensation. As the temperature of water in a gaseous state decreases, its particles lose energy and begin to move slower and clump together.

If they continue to lose energy, the water vapor will eventually turn back into a liquid.

Evaporation and condensation are also seen on a larger scale all around us. Surface water from sources like the ocean, lakes, and rivers can evaporate as energy from the sun causes its temperature to increase.

Increased evaporation results increased humidity. Humidity is a measure of the amount of gaseous water in the air.

As the gaseous water rises it will encounter colder and colder temperatures in the atmosphere. Eventually, the temperatures will become so cold that water condenses, changes into liquid form, and falls from the sky as rain.
