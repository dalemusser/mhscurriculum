The MHS Argumentation (12 items) and Water Content (23 items) Assessments were validated on a sample of 1,110 students using Rasch measurement procedures (Wulff, 2019; Reeves et al. 2020). For the Argumentation Assessment, Rasch model infit values ranged from 0.80 to 1.16, suggesting that students’ responses are not unduly influenced by factors extraneous to their own ability and the item’s difficulty. The item Rasch difficulty spread of 2.7 logits suggests that the assessment contains items suitable for measuring students at a variety of levels of argumentation ability (Wulff, 2019). For the Water Content Assessment, Rasch analysis also suggests adequate fit with the Rasch model (infit between 0.79 and 1.29). The 2.4 logit spread in item difficulty suggests that the items on the WSA provide information about students with a wide range of water systems knowledge (Wulff, 2019).

Both the Argumentation and Water Content Assessments demonstrated an internal consistency exceeding the minimum guidelines established by the What Works Clearinghouse (WWC, 2014). The Argumentation Assessment provides measures with a reliability at or above 0.6 (<sub>pre</sub> = 0.595, <sub>post</sub> = 0.673). The Water Content Assessment provides measures with a reliability at or above 0.7 (<sub>pre</sub> = 0.719, <sub>post</sub> = 0.815).

As they stand, we expect these assessments to be well-suited for evaluation of the proposed project outcomes given that they have demonstrated adequate construct validity with a large and diverse sample of middle school students. If higher reliability is needed for the proposed study, we have the option of adding more items or asking students to complete the assessment tasks multiple times in the context of different scenarios. However, since the target population in the proposed study is similar to that in the published reports (Wulff, 2019; Reeves et al. 2020), we expect the reliability estimates provided here to be robust across different samples in the proposed project.
